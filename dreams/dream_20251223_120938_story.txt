### Creative Idea: "Temporal Bias Lab"

**Concept**: Create an interactive virtual reality (VR) or augmented reality (AR) experience that simulates different environments—specifically contrasting a bustling 1920s city street and a modern shopping mall—to observe and study how these settings influence confirmation bias in decision-making processes.

#### Key Features:
1. **Historical Contrast**: Participants start in a recreated 1920s street environment, where they interact with characters and objects from that era. They then transition to a modern shopping mall setting, where they interact with similar elements but in a drastically different architectural and technological environment.

2. **Behavioral Scenarios**: Participants are given tasks or decisions that require evaluating information. For instance:
   - **1920s Street**: A detective is trying to solve a crime and must decide whom to trust based on clues given by nervous suspects and witnesses.
   - **Shopping Mall**: A consumer is looking for a product and must decide which brand to buy based on reviews, advertisements, and personal experiences.

3. **Data Collection**: The system tracks participants' choices and the factors that influence their decisions. It records how environmental cues (e.g., lighting, crowd behavior, available information) affect their likelihood of confirming pre-existing beliefs.

4. **AI Analysis**: An AI component analyzes the data collected to identify patterns and correlations between environmental factors and cognitive biases. This could include identifying which stimuli (e.g., signage, crowd behavior) most strongly reinforce confirmation bias.

5. **Educational Feedback**: After the simulation, participants receive personalized feedback on their decision-making processes, highlighting moments where confirmation bias influenced their choices. They are also shown comparative data from others to illustrate the broader impact of these biases in different settings.

#### Unique Aspect:
The interplay between historical and modern environments provides a unique lens through which to examine confirmation bias. By contrasting the 1920s street's reliance on interpersonal interactions with the modern mall's structured pathways and information overload, the experience offers insights into how environmental design can subtly influence cognitive processes.
